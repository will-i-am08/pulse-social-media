# Geekly – Brand Prompt Guidelines
**For use by Pulse Social Media AI prompt generation**

---

## 1. Brand Overview

**Location:** 37 Williamson Street, Bendigo VIC, Australia
**Established:** 2020
**Category:** Local device repair shop
**Services:** iPhone repair, MacBook repair, Android phones, tablets, laptops, Windows PCs, data recovery, virus removal, battery replacements, screen replacements, software troubleshooting

**Facebook:** facebook.com/geeklycomputers
**Instagram:** @geeklybendigo

Geekly is Bendigo's most visited smartphone and computer repair workshop. They lean proudly into their "geek" identity, positioning themselves as specialist experts who are approachable, transparent, and community-focused.

---

## 2. Brand Voice & Tone

### Core personality
- **Friendly and approachable** – never cold or corporate. Speaks like a knowledgeable mate, not a manual.
- **Confident without being arrogant** – they know their stuff and show it through helpfulness, not jargon.
- **Playful** – willing to use pop culture references, light humour, and casual language. (E.g., a hiring post referred to filling "the last member of the A-Team.")
- **Local and community-driven** – speaks directly to Bendigo residents. Uses "we" and "our" to reinforce belonging to the community.
- **Transparent** – honest about pricing, process, and capabilities. No gimmicks or hidden language.

### Tone modifiers by post type

| Post type | Tone |
|---|---|
| Service promotion | Helpful, clear, confident |
| Tips & advice | Educational but conversational, never condescending |
| Team/culture posts | Warm, personal, proud |
| Humour/memes | Light, geeky, relatable |
| Offers/promotions | Punchy, direct, exciting |
| Customer shoutouts | Grateful, warm, genuine |

## 3. Post Layout & Structure

Based on observed patterns, Geekly posts typically follow one of these layouts:

### Layout A – Hook → Detail → CTA
Best for service promotions and repair spotlights.
```
[Attention-grabbing opening line — question or bold statement]

[1–3 short sentences describing the service, problem it solves, or what to expect]

[Call to action — walk in, call, or visit website]
```
**Example structure:**
> Cracked screen? Don't stress. 📱
>
> We fix iPhone screens same day — no bookings needed, just walk in.
>
> Find us at 37 Williamson Street, Bendigo. 👇

---

### Layout B – Tip/Fact → Offer
Best for educational posts with a soft sell.
```
[Useful tip or relatable problem]

[Insight or brief explanation]

[Soft CTA linking back to the service]
```
**Example structure:**
> Your iPhone battery draining faster than usual? That's usually a sign it needs replacing.
>
> A fresh battery can feel like a brand new phone — and it costs less than you think.
>
> Pop in and we'll check it out for free.

---

### Layout C – Short & Punchy
Best for memes, humour posts, promotions, or team culture content.
```
[1–2 lines max — witty, relatable, or celebratory]

[Optional emoji or hashtags]
```
**Example structure:**
> When someone asks if their laptop is fixable and you say "absolutely." 💪
>
> #GeeklyBendigo #ComputerRepair

---

### Layout D – Story/Update
Best for team news, milestones, or hiring posts.
```
[Personal or business update — conversational opening]

[Context or backstory in 2–3 sentences]

[Invite or next step for the audience]
```
**Example structure:**
> We're looking for the last member of the A-Team at Geekly.
>
> [Role details and what you're looking for]
>
> Think you've got what it takes? Send us a message.

## 4. Key Messaging Pillars

All generated prompts should draw from one or more of these pillars:

### Pillar 1 – Transparent Pricing
Geekly charges Parts + Labour as a flat rate — never by the hour. Posts can reference this to build trust and stand out from competitors.
- Phrases to use: "no hidden fees," "flat rate," "know the price before we start," "no surprises"

### Pillar 2 – Walk-In Convenience
No bookings required. This is a key differentiator and should appear regularly in CTAs.
- Phrases to use: "just walk in," "no booking needed," "drop in anytime," "we're open now"

### Pillar 3 – Same-Day Repair
Fast turnaround is a major selling point, especially for screen and battery jobs.
- Phrases to use: "same day," "back in your hands today," "quick fix," "fast turnaround"

### Pillar 4 – Specialist Expertise
Every technician is a specialist in their field — not generalists.
- Phrases to use: "specialist technicians," "in-house repair," "we know [device] inside out," "years of experience"

### Pillar 5 – Local Community Connection
Geekly is proudly from Bendigo and serves Bendigo. Posts should feel local, not generic.
- Phrases to use: "Bendigo's #1," "your local repair team," "right here in Bendigo," "serving our community since 2020"

## 5. Topics & Content Categories

Prompts should rotate across these content types to keep the feed varied:

- **Device repair spotlights** – feature one specific repair (e.g., cracked screen, dead battery, water damage, virus removal)
- **Before/after or diagnostic stories** – walk through a repair journey (without identifying customers)
- **Tips & how-tos** – simple device care tips the audience can use immediately
- **Social proof / reviews** – share customer feedback with gratitude
- **Team culture** – introduce team members, celebrate milestones, share behind-the-scenes
- **Seasonal or timely content** – back to school, holiday device damage, end-of-year tech deals
- **Promotions** – highlight specific service deals or refurbished device stock
- **Humour/geek culture** – relatable tech memes or playful observations about device life

---

## 6. Language Rules

### Always do:
- Write in a warm, direct second person ("you," "your device," "pop in")
- Keep sentences short — 1–2 lines per paragraph in the post body
- Use casual Australian English spelling (e.g., "colour," "organise," "mum")
- Include a clear CTA in most posts
- Reference Bendigo naturally to keep it local

### Never do:
- Use heavy technical jargon without explanation
- Sound like a generic tech company or chain store
- Use overly formal language ("we are pleased to inform you…")
- Write long unbroken blocks of text
- Oversell or make unrealistic claims

## 7. Hashtag Style

Geekly uses a small, focused set of hashtags — not a wall of them. Aim for 3–6 max per post.

**Core hashtags to rotate:**
`#GeeklyBendigo` `#BendigoRepair` `#PhoneRepair` `#ComputerRepair` `#MacBookRepair` `#iPhoneRepair` `#BendigoLocal` `#Bendigo` `#ScreenRepair` `#SameDay`

Use device-specific hashtags when the post is about a specific repair. Keep hashtags at the end of the post, not woven into the copy.

## 8. Emoji Usage

Emojis are used sparingly but meaningfully — not on every line. Typically:
- 1–2 emojis per post
- Placed at the end of a sentence or paragraph
- Used to reinforce the message, not decorate it

**Common emojis for this brand:**
📱 💻 🔧 ⚡ 💪 ✅ 👇 🙌 🛠️

## 9. Prompt Generation Rules (For the App)

When generating a social media prompt for Geekly, the app should:

1. **Select a content category** from Section 5 (rotate evenly across categories)
2. **Choose a layout** from Section 3 that fits the category
3. **Anchor to at least one messaging pillar** from Section 4
4. **Apply the tone** from Section 2 matching the post type
5. **Include an Australian English CTA** referencing the walk-in or website
6. **End with 3–5 relevant hashtags** from Section 7
7. **Keep total post length to 50–120 words** for the body copy (excluding hashtags)

### Example prompt instruction for the app:
> Write a Facebook post for Geekly, a local device repair shop in Bendigo, Australia. The post should highlight same-day iPhone battery replacement. Use a friendly, casual tone with Australian spelling. Lead with a relatable problem, mention transparent flat-rate pricing and no booking required, then close with a walk-in CTA. Keep it under 100 words. Add 3–5 relevant hashtags at the end.

---

## 10. What Makes a Bad Post (Avoid These)

- Generic tech content that could be from any repair shop anywhere
- Posts without a clear local connection to Bendigo
- Long paragraphs that don't breathe
- Overly formal or corporate language
- Bullet-point heavy posts (keep it conversational, not listicle)
- Promotional posts that forget to mention the walk-in or no-booking point
- Inconsistent emojis or emoji overload
